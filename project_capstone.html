Capstone Project

Silver Design: UX for Seniors


Silver Design is a UX framework for designers to use when building new digital experiences. The framework includes resources for designers, such as usage guidelines, an evaluation checklist, and design components.

All results and data were compiled into final a usability evaluation report.

Our design framework was made into an interactive website.

Problem


As humans age, we see many changes such as vision impairment, reduced dexterity, and cognition. These limitations may affect accessibility to digital products and overall digital experience. A UI design framework for UX designers and engineers currently does not exist when designing for senior users.

Our goal is to start building a framework that will make designing for all easier and help make the web a more accessible and inclusive place.

Solution


My team conducted a Competiive Analysis, Competitive Review, Accessblity Evaluations, and User Survey to help lay the foundations of our UI design framework.

We ultimately produced a high-fidelity interactive prototype, along with a presentation and report detailing our process.

1. Problem Space & Discovery


Problem Space


  • The senior population is growing worldwide.
  • Currently, there is a lack in a UI design framework specific to the needs of senior users.
  • We aim to create a framework tailored to seniors to enhance accessibility and inclusivity in an increasingly digital world.

Competitive Analysis


Our competitive review revealed that there are no direct competitors currently in the market offering a framework for UX designers and engineers to use when designing for senior users.


Tools: Google Search, Figjam


Competitive Analysis Chart

Findings:

  • Accessibility standards such as WCAG and ARIA exist.
  • Design systems such as Material Design by Google, Human Interface Guidelines by Apple, and Fluent 2 by Microsoft exist are not specific to senior users.
  • Indirect competitors such as GrandPad, Elder Launch, AARP Tools and SeniorPlanet to inspire and refine our designs.

Literature Review


A review of current literature uncovered that there is a lot of existing peer-reviewed research on the subject of design for the elderly.

Tools: Google Scholar, Figjam

  • Guidelines related to to cognitive, touch interaction, and visual design.
  • Has provided us with research from which to work.
  • Allows us to answer the research question, “How might designers create more inclusive web experiences for senior users?”.

Affinity diagram developed from literature review

2. User Research

Senior User Survey


We surveyed 9 senior participants to understand their perspectives on web technologies and identify their pain points. They were asked to share experiences with technology they find challenging and easy in existing web and mobile experiences.

Tools: Google Forms

Findings:

  • 8 of 9 participants use their smartphone daily.
  • 77.8% regularly use email, shop, reveiw news websites/apps online.
  • 55% felt confident using technology and believed that they can figure out how to use new applications or devices on their own
  • 55% indicated that they often need help from others to use technology
  • 33% found technology to be too complicated.
  • Over 88% emphasized simple navigation and clear instructions.


Snippet of survey results

Designer Survey

We surveyed 10 classmate to recall when a senior family/friend interacted with a digital device to gain an outside perspective that the target demographic may not report.

Tools: Google Forms

Findings:

  • Pain points identified include hidden functionality or unclear paths, difficulty adjusting or learning to new updates or devices, and small text size.
  • Participants expressed that clear labels, simple interface design, and voice commands would improve accessibility for seniors.

Accessbility Evaluation

Based on our findings of the Senior User Survey, we chose to evaluate travel sites to gain insight for our design.

We conducted web accessibility evaluations on four travel sites, Kayak, Momondo, Southwest Airlines, and United Airlines.

Tools: TAW at AAA Standards, WCAG Contrast Checker

Findings:

  • Unlabeled icons were the most frequent issue.
  • Most sites had text smaller than 12pt.
  • Clickable target areas smaller than 44px were also observed.
  • Most sites lacked spacing between selectable areas of at least 24px.

Issues found during this accessbility evaluation were compiled and annotated onto an inspiration board to serve as the groundwork for our UI component.

4. Usability Evaluation

Usability Evaluation


We conducted a moderated usability evaluation with 4 participants on 2 tasks. Each seesion was conducted and recorded via Zoom, transcribed and archieved in the team's Google Drive.



Usability session participant data table

Tasks


  1. Search for a Flight

    Users should input the information needed to successfully search for a flight. This task is successful when a user can easily add the correct information into the text field, initiate the flight search, and find a preferred flight.

  2. Locate customer service chat

    Users can navigate to the customer service page. The task is successful when the chat bot is located.

Results


Task 1

  • 4 of 4 participants completed the task.
  • 2 of 4 participants had two errors.
  • Mean (average) completion time was 114 seconds.

Most participants had issues locating the "Select Date" and exit button on the calendar.


Task 2

  • 3 of 4 participants completed the task.
  • 4 of 4 participants had at least one error.
  • Mean (average) completion time was 155 seconds.

Participants felt that there were too many places to find help or information that were often labeled with different terminology.

Participants consistently focused on the the top third of the page and stated some pages had too much content.

One particpant used the flight search button to search the entire site.

5. Prototype Iteration

Iterations


Based on the feedback we recieved from our 4 partcipants, these are some of the changes we made to our prototype.

Task 1

  • The calendar component was revised to display one month at a time.
  • “Select Date” and “Exit” buttons were placed on their own line
  • Text within the input fields of the flight search were darkened and bolded to increase readability.

Task 2

  • Relabled button to “Find a Flight” to ensure clarity of the button functionality.
  • Terminology consolidated for consistency throughout.
